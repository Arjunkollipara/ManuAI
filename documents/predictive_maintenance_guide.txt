Predictive Maintenance Guide: Vibration and Sensor Monitoring

Purpose: This guide explains how maintenance and reliability teams should interpret sensor data from industrial motors, compressors, turbines, CNC machines, and rotating production assets. Predictive maintenance uses measured condition trends to schedule work before failures create quality loss, downtime, or safety risk.

Key sensors: Temperature sensors measure motor winding, bearing housing, ambient air, coolant, and process temperature. Accelerometers measure vibration velocity, acceleration, and bearing fault frequencies. Tachometers or encoder feedback measure rotational speed. Torque sensors or drive estimates measure mechanical load. Current sensors measure electrical demand and phase imbalance. Oil sensors track moisture, particles, and viscosity. Tool wear sensors estimate cutting edge degradation from spindle load, vibration, and part quality indicators.

Normal operating ranges: Ambient air around equipment should typically remain between 15 C and 40 C. Motor and bearing surface temperatures should stabilize below 70 C, with caution above 80 C. Process air or compressor discharge temperature should normally remain below 105 C. Rotational speed should remain within plus or minus 5 percent of the operating setpoint unless a VFD recipe permits a wider range. Torque should normally remain below 85 percent of rated load during steady production. Tool wear should remain below 60 percent for normal operation; 60-80 percent requires planning, and above 80 percent requires scheduled replacement.

Warning thresholds: Investigate vibration above 4.5 mm/s RMS on most medium industrial machines, above 7.1 mm/s RMS for severe service, or any sudden increase greater than 25 percent from baseline. Temperature rise greater than 10 C in 30 minutes, torque above 90 percent rated load, speed instability over 5 percent, or tool wear above 80 percent should trigger maintenance review. Critical alarms include bearing temperature above 100 C, compressor discharge temperature above 120 C, vibration above 11 mm/s RMS, or repeated current overload trips.

Interpreting trends: Single readings are useful, but trend direction matters more. A slow temperature increase with rising vibration often indicates bearing lubrication breakdown. Rising torque with stable speed may indicate process restriction, dull tooling, or mechanical drag. High vibration at running speed suggests imbalance; vibration at two times running speed often indicates misalignment. Spikes that coincide with product changeovers may be process-related and should be compared with recipe, load, and operator notes.

Escalation matrix: LOW risk means values remain inside normal range and trends are flat; continue routine monitoring. MEDIUM means one warning threshold is exceeded or trend slope is increasing; schedule inspection within seven days. HIGH means multiple warning thresholds are exceeded, defect frequencies appear, or risk score is 70-89; schedule maintenance within 24-48 hours. CRITICAL means shutdown limits are exceeded, risk score is 90 or above, or safety symptoms are present; stop equipment and notify the senior technician and production supervisor.

Maintenance scheduling and records: Use risk scores to prioritize weekly planning, spare parts staging, and downtime windows. Record raw readings, baselines, charts, work orders, technician findings, replaced parts, and post-maintenance verification so future models can learn from confirmed failure modes.
